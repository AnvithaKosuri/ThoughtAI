Overview

Problem framing, solution approach, and what we learned from needfinding.

!

Problem

People across many backgrounds increasingly view AI with skepticism. Many AI tools provide shortcuts that discourage critical thinking and replace human effort rather than supporting it. As companies adopt AI for speed and efficiency, tools often prioritize output over understanding. This has led to concern that human reasoning and problem solving are being devalued.

In education, this tension is especially visible. Students use AI to bypass reasoning, receive full solutions instantly, and disengage from the learning process. Our GR5 testing showed that while students want help, they do not want to lose control of their thinking. The challenge is designing AI that supports learning without replacing human reasoning.

↗

Solution

Instead of providing answers on demand, the AI withholds full solutions unless the student demonstrates active thinking. Progress is earned through visible reasoning, partial work, and engagement with guided prompts. The AI responds with hints, questions, and feedback rather than final answers, reinforcing learning without replacing human thought. This approach reframes AI as a support for reasoning rather than a shortcut.

Guided prompts Visible reasoning User control
▦

Product

ThoughtAI is an iPad based learning app that applies this solution to handwritten problem solving.

Students can write directly on the screen or take photos of their work. ThoughtAI provides 3 forms of analyzing their reasoning and delivers contextual guidance, requiring students to complete each reasoning step before progressing or requesting further help.

By integrating AI into a student’s natural workflow, ThoughtAI supports reflection and understanding while preventing over reliance on AI generated answers.

✓

What we learned (needfinding)

  • Across users, autonomy without visibility reduced trust.
  • People preferred AI that supports learning rather than replaces thinking.
  • Users wanted clear boundaries and control over how help is given.
  • Transparency mattered more than “flashy” interaction: users wanted reasoning, uncertainty cues, and the ability to verify.
  • Overreliance felt likely under stress, so help should default to low intervention with clear escalation paths.
  • Users responded better to calm, efficient guidance than overly human-like or chatty behavior.
